3  Graph Theory

The mathematical language of network structure

3.1 Introduction

Network science rests on a mathematical foundation: graph theory. Before studying random graphs, small worlds, or scale-free networks, it is essential to establish the formal language used to describe networks. That language is graph theory.

Graph theory is a branch of discrete mathematics that studies collections of objects and the pairwise connections between them. Its origins trace back to Leonhard Euler’s 1736 solution to the Königsberg bridge problem, in which the question of whether a particular city could be traversed by crossing each of its seven bridges exactly once was reformulated as a purely combinatorial question about a drawing made of vertices and edges. Euler’s insight — that only the pattern of connections, not the physical geography, determines the answer — is the founding idea of graph theory (Euler 1736).

In the context of network science, graph theory provides a common mathematical language for later topics such as random graphs, small-world structure, and scale-free degree heterogeneity (Diestel 2017; West 2001; Newman 2010). Specifically, it provides:

  • a precise vocabulary for describing structures,
  • algebraic representations that enable computation,
  • combinatorial results that govern global properties,
  • and a rigorous basis for the probabilistic models studied in later chapters.

3.2 Learning goals

By the end of this chapter, it should be possible to:

  • define a graph, its vertices and edges, and give the standard notation
  • distinguish between undirected, directed, weighted, and multigraphs
  • define degree, degree sequence, and prove the handshaking lemma
  • construct the adjacency matrix and the Laplacian matrix
  • define walks, paths, trails, and cycles precisely
  • define connectivity, connected components, cut vertices, and bridges
  • define trees, spanning trees, and identify their key properties
  • identify bipartite graphs and apply König’s criterion
  • describe standard graph families and recognise them
  • use igraph in R to construct and analyse graphs

3.3 Chapter roadmap

This chapter moves from the most basic objects to the more structural concepts needed later in the book. It begins with the definition of a graph and the main graph types, then studies degree and matrix representations, and only after that turns to walks, connectivity, trees, bipartite structure, and standard graph families. The chapter ends with a compact computational toolkit in R, so that each theoretical definition is connected to a concrete representation that can be explored computationally.

3.4 What is a graph?

Formal definition

Definition 3.1 A graph G is an ordered pair

G = (V, E)

where V is a finite non-empty set called the vertex set (or node set), and E is a set of unordered pairs \{u, v\} with u, v \in V and u \neq v, called the edge set.

The number of vertices is denoted by N = |V| and is called the order of the graph. The number of edges is denoted by M = |E| and is called the size of the graph.

Each element of E is an unordered pair \{u, v\}, also written as uv or (u,v) when direction is irrelevant. The vertices u and v are said to be adjacent or neighbors, and the edge uv is said to be incident to both u and v.

NoteConvention

Throughout this chapter, graphs are assumed to be simple unless stated otherwise. A simple graph has no self-loops (edges from a vertex to itself) and no multi-edges (more than one edge between the same pair of vertices).

A motivating example

Consider a graph with vertex set V = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\} and edge set

E = \bigl\{\{1,2\},\, \{1,3\},\, \{2,4\},\, \{3,4\},\, \{4,5\}\bigr\}.

This graph has order N = 5 and size M = 5. Vertices 1, 2, and 3 are each reachable from vertex 4 through paths of length one or two, while vertex 5 hangs off vertex 4.

3.5 Directed and undirected graphs

The definition above assumes that each edge is unordered: if u and v are connected, the connection runs symmetrically in both directions. Such a graph is called undirected.

In many real networks, connections are asymmetric. A hyperlink may point from page u to page v without the reverse link existing. A neuron may send signals to a target cell without receiving signals back.

Definition 3.2 A directed graph (or digraph) is a pair G = (V, A) where V is the vertex set and A is a set of ordered pairs (u, v) with u \neq v, called arcs or directed edges.

The arc (u, v) goes from u to v. The vertex u is called the tail and v is called the head. The arc (u, v) and the arc (v, u) are distinct objects in a directed graph.

Weighted graphs

Definition 3.3 A weighted graph is a graph G = (V, E) together with a weight function w : E \to \mathbb{R}. Each edge e \in E is assigned a real-valued weight w(e).

Weights may represent distances, capacities, interaction strengths, or costs, depending on the application.

3.6 Degree and the handshaking lemma

Degree of a vertex

Definition 3.4 Let G = (V, E) be an undirected graph. The degree of a vertex v \in V, denoted k_v or \deg(v), is the number of edges incident to v:

k_v = |\{u \in V : uv \in E\}|.

A vertex of degree 0 is called isolated. A vertex of degree 1 is called a leaf or pendant vertex. The maximum degree in the graph is denoted \Delta(G) and the minimum degree is denoted \delta(G).

Degree sequence

Definition 3.5 The degree sequence of a graph G is the list of degrees of all vertices, written in non-increasing order:

k_1 \geq k_2 \geq \cdots \geq k_N.

The degree sequence is a fundamental invariant of the graph. It captures the distribution of connections across all vertices.

The handshaking lemma

Theorem 3.1 Handshaking Lemma. For any undirected graph G = (V, E),

\sum_{v \in V} k_v = 2M.

Proof. Each edge \{u, v\} \in E contributes exactly 1 to the degree of u and exactly 1 to the degree of v. Summing over all vertices therefore counts each edge exactly twice. \square

NoteImmediate corollary

The number of vertices with odd degree is always even. This follows because the sum of all degrees equals 2M, which is even, and an even sum requires an even number of odd summands.

Average degree

Definition 3.6 The average degree of G is

\langle k \rangle = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{v \in V} k_v = \frac{2M}{N}.

The identity \langle k \rangle = 2M/N follows immediately from the handshaking lemma. It connects average degree, edge count, and graph order in one simple formula that will appear repeatedly throughout this book.

In-degree and out-degree in directed graphs

In a directed graph, each arc contributes to the degree of two vertices in an asymmetric way.

Definition 3.7 Let G = (V, A) be a directed graph and v \in V. The in-degree of v is

k_v^{\text{in}} = |\{u \in V : (u, v) \in A\}|

and the out-degree of v is

k_v^{\text{out}} = |\{u \in V : (v, u) \in A\}|.

The directed analogue of the handshaking lemma states:

\sum_{v \in V} k_v^{\text{in}} = \sum_{v \in V} k_v^{\text{out}} = |A|.

This holds because every arc (u, v) contributes exactly 1 to the out-degree of u and exactly 1 to the in-degree of v.

3.7 Matrix representations

Matrix representations are especially important in network science because they translate combinatorial structure into linear-algebraic objects. This makes it possible to study connectivity, diffusion, centrality, and spectral structure with computational tools that extend far beyond hand-drawn examples (Newman 2010).

The adjacency matrix

Definition 3.8 Let G = (V, E) be a graph with V = \{1, 2, \ldots, N\}. The adjacency matrix of G is the N \times N matrix A defined by

A_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \{i, j\} \in E, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}

For an undirected graph, the adjacency matrix is symmetric: A_{ij} = A_{ji} for all i, j. For a simple graph, all diagonal entries are zero: A_{ii} = 0.

Key properties of the adjacency matrix

Several immediate properties follow from the definition:

  • The row sum of row i equals the degree of vertex i: \sum_{j} A_{ij} = k_i.
  • The total sum of all entries equals twice the number of edges: \sum_{i,j} A_{ij} = 2M.
  • The entry (A^r)_{ij} of the r-th power of A counts the number of walks of length r from vertex i to vertex j.

That last property is particularly powerful. It means that matrix exponentiation yields combinatorial information about paths in the graph.

The degree matrix

Definition 3.9 The degree matrix of G is the N \times N diagonal matrix D where D_{ii} = k_i and D_{ij} = 0 for i \neq j.

The Laplacian matrix

Definition 3.10 The Laplacian matrix of G is

L = D - A.

The Laplacian is symmetric and positive semi-definite. Its entries are:

L_{ij} = \begin{cases} k_i & \text{if } i = j, \\ -1 & \text{if } \{i, j\} \in E, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}

The smallest eigenvalue of L is always 0, with the all-ones vector \mathbf{1} as eigenvector. The multiplicity of the zero eigenvalue equals the number of connected components. The second smallest eigenvalue, known as the algebraic connectivity or Fiedler value \lambda_2, measures how well-connected the graph is: larger \lambda_2 implies greater robustness to edge removal.

3.8 Walks, paths, and cycles

Walk

Definition 3.11 A walk in a graph G = (V, E) is a finite sequence of vertices

v_0, v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_\ell

such that \{v_{i-1}, v_i\} \in E for every i = 1, \ldots, \ell. The integer \ell is the length of the walk.

A walk may repeat vertices and edges. It simply requires that consecutive vertices in the sequence are adjacent.

Trail and path

Definition 3.12 A trail is a walk in which no edge is repeated. A path is a walk in which no vertex is repeated (and therefore no edge is repeated either).

The path is the strictest concept: it visits each vertex at most once. A trail relaxes this to edges only. Euler’s original Königsberg question was precisely about the existence of a closed trail — a trail that returns to its starting vertex, now called an Eulerian circuit.

Cycle

Definition 3.13 A cycle is a closed walk v_0, v_1, \ldots, v_\ell = v_0 of length \ell \geq 3 in which all vertices v_0, v_1, \ldots, v_{\ell-1} are distinct.

The girth of a graph is the length of its shortest cycle. A graph with no cycles is called acyclic.

NoteConnection to matrix powers

Recall that (A^r)_{ij} counts walks of length r from i to j. In particular, the diagonal entry (A^3)_{ii} counts closed walks of length 3 starting and ending at i. The number of triangles in G is therefore

\text{triangles}(G) = \frac{1}{6} \operatorname{tr}(A^3).

The factor 1/6 accounts for the six ways to traverse each triangle (two directions \times three starting vertices).

3.9 Connectivity

Connected graphs and components

Definition 3.14 An undirected graph G is connected if for every pair of distinct vertices u, v \in V, there exists a path from u to v.

If G is not connected, it decomposes into maximal connected subgraphs, called connected components.

Definition 3.15 A connected component of G is a maximal connected subgraph of G.

The number of connected components is denoted c(G). Note that c(G) = 1 if and only if G is connected.

Strong and weak connectivity in directed graphs

For directed graphs, two notions of connectivity arise.

Definition 3.16 A directed graph is strongly connected if for every ordered pair (u, v) there exists a directed path from u to v. It is weakly connected if the underlying undirected graph is connected.

Strong connectivity requires reachability in both directions between every pair of vertices. Weak connectivity only requires that edge directions can be ignored.

Cut vertices and bridges

Two special structural elements are important for understanding how connectivity can be disrupted.

Definition 3.17 A cut vertex (or articulation point) is a vertex v whose removal increases the number of connected components:

c(G - v) > c(G).

Definition 3.18 A bridge is an edge e whose removal increases the number of connected components:

c(G - e) > c(G).

Cut vertices and bridges are the most vulnerable elements of a connected graph. Their removal disconnects the network. Identifying them has direct relevance to network robustness, which is examined in detail in Chapter 7.

3.10 Trees and spanning trees

Definition of a tree

Definition 3.19 A tree is a connected acyclic graph.

Trees are among the most important and well-understood graph families. Several equivalent characterizations exist.

Theorem 3.2 Theorem. Let G be a graph with N vertices. The following statements are equivalent:

  1. G is a tree.
  2. G is connected and has exactly N - 1 edges.
  3. G is acyclic and has exactly N - 1 edges.
  4. Any two distinct vertices of G are connected by exactly one path.
  5. G is connected, but removing any single edge disconnects it.

The equivalence of these statements is a classical result. In particular, M = N - 1 is a necessary and sufficient condition for a connected graph to be a tree. A forest is an acyclic graph that is not necessarily connected; each connected component of a forest is a tree.

Spanning trees

Definition 3.20 A spanning tree of a connected graph G = (V, E) is a subgraph T = (V, E') with E' \subseteq E that is itself a tree — that is, it contains all N vertices with exactly N - 1 edges, is connected, and is acyclic.

Every connected graph contains at least one spanning tree. When edge weights are present, the minimum spanning tree is the spanning tree for which the total edge weight is minimized. The Kruskal and Prim algorithms find minimum spanning trees in O(M \log N) time.

3.11 Bipartite graphs

Definition

Definition 3.21 A graph G = (V, E) is bipartite if the vertex set can be partitioned into two non-empty disjoint sets V = X \cup Y such that every edge in E has one endpoint in X and one endpoint in Y. The partition (X, Y) is called the bipartition.

In a bipartite graph, there are no edges within X and no edges within Y. All edges cross the bipartition. Bipartite graphs arise naturally in models of two-mode networks: actors and films, employees and projects, genes and diseases.

König’s criterion

Theorem 3.3 Theorem (König, 1916). A graph G is bipartite if and only if it contains no cycle of odd length.

This criterion is both elegant and computationally useful. Bipartiteness can be detected by a simple two-coloring of the graph, which runs in O(N + M) time using breadth-first search.

Complete bipartite graph

Definition 3.22 The complete bipartite graph K_{m,n} is the bipartite graph with parts |X| = m and |Y| = n in which every vertex in X is adjacent to every vertex in Y. It has mn edges and its degree sequence consists of n vertices of degree m and m vertices of degree n.

3.12 Special graph families

Several families appear repeatedly in network science as benchmarks and building blocks.

Complete graph

Definition 3.23 The complete graph K_N is the simple graph on N vertices in which every pair of distinct vertices is adjacent. It has

M = \binom{N}{2} = \frac{N(N-1)}{2}

edges and every vertex has degree N - 1.

Cycle and path graphs

Definition 3.24 The cycle graph C_N is the graph consisting of a single cycle through all N vertices. Each vertex has degree 2 and the graph has N edges.

Definition 3.25 The path graph P_N is the graph on N vertices forming a single path. The two endpoints have degree 1, all interior vertices have degree 2, and the graph has N - 1 edges.

Regular graphs

Definition 3.26 A graph is k-regular if every vertex has exactly degree k.

The complete graph K_N is (N-1)-regular. The cycle graph C_N is 2-regular. The Petersen graph is a famous 3-regular graph used as a counterexample in many graph-theoretic conjectures.

3.13 Subgraphs and graph isomorphism

Subgraph

Definition 3.27 A graph H = (V', E') is a subgraph of G = (V, E) if V' \subseteq V and E' \subseteq E. It is an induced subgraph on V' \subseteq V if E' contains exactly all edges of G with both endpoints in V'.

The induced subgraph on a vertex set S is written G[S].

Graph isomorphism

Definition 3.28 Two graphs G = (V_1, E_1) and H = (V_2, E_2) are isomorphic, written G \cong H, if there exists a bijection \phi : V_1 \to V_2 such that

\{u, v\} \in E_1 \iff \{\phi(u), \phi(v)\} \in E_2.

The function \phi is called a graph isomorphism.

Isomorphic graphs are structurally identical: one is simply a relabeling of the other. Isomorphism captures the idea that only the pattern of connections — not the labels on vertices — defines a graph’s structure.

A necessary condition for isomorphism is that two graphs have the same degree sequence. This condition is easy to check but is not sufficient: non-isomorphic graphs can share the same degree sequence.

3.16 Exercises

Part I. Computational and derivation exercises

Exercise 1. Handshaking lemma

Let G be an undirected simple graph with N = 8 vertices and degree sequence (5, 4, 4, 3, 3, 2, 1, 0).

  1. Verify that the degree sequence satisfies the handshaking lemma.
  2. Determine the number of edges M.
  3. Compute the average degree \langle k \rangle.

Exercise 2. Adjacency matrix

Let G have vertex set \{1,2,3,4\} and edge set \{\{1,2\},\{1,3\},\{2,3\},\{3,4\}\}.

  1. Write the adjacency matrix A of G.
  2. Compute A^2 by hand.
  3. Identify what the diagonal entries of A^2 represent in terms of walks.
  4. Compute the number of triangles using \operatorname{tr}(A^3) and verify with igraph.

Exercise 3. Laplacian eigenvalues

For the graph in Exercise 2:

  1. Construct the degree matrix D and the Laplacian L = D - A.
  2. Compute the eigenvalues of L.
  3. Verify that the smallest eigenvalue is 0 with eigenvector \mathbf{1}.
  4. Interpret the second-smallest eigenvalue.

Exercise 4. Trees

  1. Prove that any tree on N vertices has exactly N - 1 edges.
  2. How many distinct labeled trees exist on N = 4 vertices? Draw them all.
  3. Give an example of a connected graph that is not a tree, and remove one edge to obtain a spanning tree.

Exercise 5. Bipartite graphs

  1. Draw K_{2,3} and compute its number of edges.
  2. Verify using König’s criterion that K_{2,3} contains no odd cycle.
  3. Show that the cycle graph C_5 is not bipartite.
  4. For which values of N is the cycle graph C_N bipartite?

Exercise 6. Cut vertices and bridges

For a path graph P_N with N \geq 3:

  1. Identify all cut vertices.
  2. Identify all bridges.
  3. What changes if P_N is replaced by the cycle C_N?
  4. Argue that any edge of a tree is a bridge.

Part II. Conceptual and analytical exercises

Exercise 7. Degree sequence realizability

A sequence of non-negative integers (d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_N) is called graphical if it is the degree sequence of some simple graph.

  1. Show that (3, 3, 2, 2, 1, 1) is graphical by constructing a graph.
  2. Show that (4, 3, 2, 2, 1) is not graphical using the handshaking lemma.
  3. State the Erdős–Gallai theorem and apply it to check whether (3, 3, 2, 2, 2) is graphical.

Exercise 8. Isomorphism

  1. Determine whether the cycle C_6 and the graph consisting of two disjoint triangles are isomorphic. Use degree sequences and triangle counts as invariants.
  2. Construct an explicit isomorphism or prove that none exists.

Exercise 9. Powers of the adjacency matrix

Let G = C_4 (the 4-cycle with vertices arranged cyclically).

  1. Write the adjacency matrix A.
  2. Compute A^2 and A^3.
  3. Use the entries of A^2 to identify all walks of length 2 between non-adjacent pairs.
  4. Explain why (A^r)_{ij} counts walks, not paths.

Exercise 10. Regular graphs

  1. Show that in a k-regular graph with N vertices, the number of edges is M = kN/2.
  2. Deduce that kN must be even. State a condition on k and N guaranteeing this.
  3. Show that the eigenvalues of the adjacency matrix of a k-regular graph all lie in the interval [-k, k], with k itself always an eigenvalue.

Part III. Simulation and coding exercises

Exercise 11. The Petersen graph

Using igraph:

  1. Construct the Petersen graph with graph.famous("Petersen").
  2. Report its order, size, degree sequence, diameter, girth, and clustering coefficient.
  3. Plot it with vertex size proportional to degree.
  4. Compute its Laplacian eigenvalues. Verify that the Fiedler value is positive.

Exercise 12. Exploring graph families

For each of K_5, C_{10}, P_8, and a random G(20, 0.25) graph:

  1. Compute the degree sequence, average degree, diameter, and clustering coefficient.
  2. Determine whether the graph is bipartite using is_bipartite().
  3. Find a spanning tree with mst() and plot it alongside the original graph.

Exercise 13. Adjacency matrix powers

Write an R function that, given a graph G and an integer r, returns the matrix A^r and uses it to count:

  1. All walks of length 3 in C_5.
  2. The number of triangles in a random G(15, 0.3) graph.

Verify both results using igraph’s built-in functions.

Exercise 14. Detecting cut vertices

Write an R function that:

  1. Generates a random connected graph on N vertices.
  2. Identifies all cut vertices using articulation_points().
  3. Visualizes the graph with cut vertices highlighted in a contrasting color.
  4. Repeats this across a grid of values of p from 0.1 to 0.6 and plots how the mean number of cut vertices changes with p.

Exercise 15. Minimum spanning tree on a weighted graph

Generate a random graph on 12 vertices. Assign edge weights sampled uniformly from [1, 10].

  1. Find the minimum spanning tree using mst().
  2. Compute and report the total weight of the MST versus the full graph.
  3. Visualize the MST edges highlighted on the full graph.
  4. Repeat with weights from an exponential distribution and compare.

3.18 Bridge to the next chapter

This chapter has treated graphs as deterministic mathematical objects: the vertex set and edge set are specified, and the goal is to reason about the resulting structure. The next chapter changes that viewpoint. Instead of fixing one graph in advance, we will study probability models that generate many possible graphs. The language introduced here — degree, adjacency matrix, connectedness, trees, and components — will become the vocabulary used to describe what a random graph typically looks like.

3.19 Concluding remarks

Graph theory provides the rigorous language that network science depends on. The definitions introduced in this chapter — graphs, adjacency matrices, degrees, paths, connectivity, trees — are not merely formal conventions. They are the precise tools that make it possible to state questions clearly, derive results exactly, and implement algorithms efficiently.

Several key ideas run through this chapter:

  • a graph encodes only the pattern of connections, not geometric or physical information,
  • the adjacency matrix links combinatorial structure to linear algebra,
  • the handshaking lemma is a universal constraint on any graph,
  • connectivity, trees, and bipartiteness are global properties that determine much of a graph’s character,
  • the Laplacian matrix connects graph structure to spectral properties, with the Fiedler value measuring robustness.

These foundations will be used throughout all subsequent chapters. The Erdős–Rényi model, the Watts–Strogatz model, and the Barabási–Albert model are all built on top of the graph-theoretic language established here. Before asking how likely a graph with certain properties is, one must first be able to describe those properties precisely — and that is what this chapter has provided.
